# Bayt al-Hikma Archive

A design system for objects that are meant to be touched, cut, folded, and held under candlelight — not looked at from a distance. The premise: every prop in this set is a page that escaped the real archive of the House of Wisdom in Baghdad, 215 AH, and survived eleven centuries in a drawer before reaching the table tonight. Nothing here is decorative for its own sake; the aging, the gold, the geometry all carry information the way the original objects would have.

**Palette.** Parchment cream (#EBDFBF) as the paper itself — never pure white, because nothing in this archive is new. Ink-walnut (#2A1B10) for all reading text, dense enough to survive a home printer's toner without washing out. Burnt umber (#6B4423) for the aging — vignettes, foxing, candle-soot at the corners — applied with the same restraint a conservator would use, present everywhere but never loud. Gold leaf (#B8862E), reserved for the few elements that were precious enough to gild: borders that mark a threshold, the seal, the astrolabe's rim. Deep emerald (#055C3F), the single color that ties this paper archive back to the House's own colors — used sparingly, only where the same hand that built the digital Bayt al-Hikma would have signed the physical one. Seal-red (#7A2E1D), reserved entirely for the one wax seal in the set.

**Type.** Three voices, never mixed. Italiana carries the display register — the lapidary register, the words you would carve rather than write, used only for titles and numerals, never for a sentence longer than four words. Crimson Pro Italic is the poetic voice — the five riddling tablets, the parchment, the final manuscript — the register of someone speaking aloud, in character, by candlelight. Lora is the documentary voice — the hadiths, the instructions, anything meant to be read for information rather than felt. IBM Plex Mono is the archivist's hand — small, precise, reserved for coordinates, cut-lines, and reference numbers, the only typeface allowed to look like it was added by a cataloguer rather than a scribe.

**Craft over abstraction.** This archive does not whisper a hidden reference for those who already know the story — every object says exactly what it is, because a tablet a child can't read is a tablet that breaks the game. The discipline here is not concealment but precision: every word a player needs is present, every coordinate a cutter needs is exact to the millimetre, every grid lines up with its twin on the facing page. Restraint is spent on what is left out — no ornament that competes with a poem, no flourish near a number that must be counted correctly — so that the one signature device, an eight-point khatim star stamped at the foot of every page, is the only thing repeated across all eleven pages, the mark of a single hand having made the whole archive in one sitting.

**Construction.** Every page is built for the home printer and the kitchen table, not the screen: real A4 and A5 dimensions, registration crosses where two pages must align under a blade, dashed lines where scissors go and solid lines where they must not. The aging is generated, not painted by eye, so the same burn pattern can recur across eleven pages without ever repeating identically — the way real foxing never repeats. Where the brief calls for legibility, legibility wins over atmosphere every time; the atmosphere is carried entirely by the paper itself, the gold, and the one mark every page shares.
